Data Cleaning, Profiling, and Exploratory Analysis (Part 1 Report)
1. Dataset Overview and Missing Data JustificationThe raw incoming dataset contains tracking features spanning time-series indices, target metrics, and repeating classification strings. Our initialization phase isolated missing values across individual columns.
Choice of Central Tendency MeasureFor columns under the 20% null rate, missing records are filled with the column median rather than the mean.The mean calculation incorporates all observations across a variable. Consequently, a handful of extreme structural outliers can easily pull the mean away from the true center of the distribution.The median splits the ranked data points exactly at the 50th percentile. This protects our imputation against extreme outliers, preventing downstream distortion of model baseline values.
2. Mathematical Impact of Feature Distribution Skewness
During statistics profiling, Feature_Skewed displayed the highest absolute skewness score.Understanding Skewness DirectionsPositive (Right) Skewness: The distribution's tail extends further toward high values. The bulk of observations cluster at lower values. For imputation, the mean is pulled upward by these extreme right-side outliers. Using the mean would artificially inflate the imputed values for typical missing data rows.Negative (Left) Skewness: The distribution's tail stretches leftward toward low values. The bulk of data points cluster at higher values. For imputation, the mean is pulled downward by these extreme low-side outliers.Because of this asymmetric pulling effect, the median serves as a far more representative central tendency metric across highly skewed variables.
3. Outlier Profile Assessment (IQR Analysis)
Using the Interquartile Range ($IQR = Q_3 - Q_1$), structural threshold limits were locked at:$$\text{Lower Bound} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times IQR$$$$\text{Upper Bound} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times IQR$$Outlier Handling PolicyRather than deleting outliers arbitrarily (which risks destroying legitimate extreme signals, such as market jumps or rare operational flags), we retain these values raw during this initial cleaning phase. For the upcoming modeling step in Part 2, these extreme values will be stabilized using Winsorization (capping at the 1st and 99th percentiles) or passed into robust scaling profiles. This approach protects downstream estimators while preserving the underlying row data structure.

5. Matrix Multi-Collinearity and Confounding Variables
The Pearson heat map isolated a high linear dependency between Feature_Linear and Feature_Nonlinear.While this shows strong mathematical tracking, correlation does not imply a direct causal relationship. A third, unobserved variable could drive both metrics. For example, if both columns represent different operational cost points, a third confounding variable—such as overall Factory Production Scale or Inflation Cycles—could move both values up or down together, creating a strong correlation without either feature directly causing the other.
6. Monotonicity Comparison (Pearson vs. Spearman)Linear vs. Monotonic Relationships
The comparison between standard Pearson coefficients and non-parametric Spearman rank evaluations highlights the nature of relationships across our features:Feature_Linear vs Feature_Nonlinear: Shows a clear variance where $\vert{}Spearman\vert{} > \vert{}Pearson\vert{}$. This indicates a relationship that is monotonic but non-linear—the variables consistently move together in the same direction, but their rate of change varies.Standard Variables: Where $\vert{}Pearson\vert{} \ge \vert{}Spearman\vert{}$, the relationship closely follows a straight linear path.Feature Selection Strategy for Part 2We will rely primarily on the Spearman Rank Correlation matrix to guide feature selection. Because standard Pearson correlation looks only for straight-line dependencies, it can miss highly predictive feature relationships that follow curve patterns. Using Spearman ensures we capture these non-linear structures, allowing us to retain important variables before building downstream models.
